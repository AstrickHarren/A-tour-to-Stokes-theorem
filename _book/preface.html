A Tour to Stoke’s theorem

Preface

Elementary analysis students are often mysteriously introduced to the Stokes theorem: in its elegant form

\[∫_{∂Ω} ω = ∫_Ω dω,\]

\(ω\) denotes one of the ill-defined differential forms, the integral is taken over an equally ill-defined surface \(Ω\), or manifold when speaking of higher dimensions, and \(∂Ω\) is supposed to its again ill-defined boundary. As if ill-defined concepts are not enough, proofs of such a theorem can be found in introductory courses as well. As hard as instructors try, the ill-supported knowledge typically remains vague in the minds of math students and henceforth serves as a bane to those who wish to refine their understanding on the famous theorem.
